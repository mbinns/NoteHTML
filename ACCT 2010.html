Corporate Ownership

  • Shares of stock can be purchased in small amounts
  • Ownership interests are transferable
  • Stockholders are not liable for the corporations debts
  • More than half of corporations in America are incorporated in Deleware due to lax laws
  • Most basic form of ownership is Common Stock:
    • Voiting rights
    • Dividends
    • Residual Claims
    • Preemptive rights

Equity v Debt Financing

  • Equit financing is long-term loans that come from issuing new common stock:
    • Advantages:
      • Does not have to be repaid
      • Dividends are optional
  • Debit financing is long-term loans that come from borrowing from lenders:
    • Advantages:
      • Interest on debt is tax deductible
      • Debt does not change stockholder control

Common Stock Transactions

  • These are balence sheet accounts only
  • Contributed capital:
    • Ammount of capital that the company recived from investors
  • Retained ernings
  • Treasury Stock:
    • Shares that have been repurchased by the company
  • Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income/Loss

Authorization, issuance, and Repurchase of Stock

  • A Corporations charter indicated the maximum numbber of shares of stock that the corporation is allowed to issue
  • Trasury stock is negative when it represents shares that are no longer outstanding with investors:
    • Durring time this is held by the company they do not carry voting/dividened/other stockholder rights
  • Outstanding shares are shares not owned by the company

Stock Authorization

  • Before stock can be issued, its specific rights and characteristics must be authorized and defined in the corporate charter. This does not affect the accounting records, but may effect how things are recorded
  • Par Value:
    • Typically has little meaning today, some states set a token ammount (usually .01$ per share) some other states allow the par value to be 0
  • Stock issuance:
    • Distributes shares to new or existing stockholders usually in exchange for cash
    • The very first issuing is called an IPO (Going public)
    • Pas the IPO additional issuings are called seasoned new issues

Stock Exchanged between Investors

  • Sales between owners of the stock are not recorded on the companies books

Stock Used To Compensate Employees

  • Employees can buy stock at a predetermined price that is not influenced by current market price

Repurchase of Stock

  • Cost method:
    • Most companies record treasury stock using the cost incurred to acquire its shares 
       National Beverage repurchased 50,000 shares of its stock for $25 per share (50,000 shares × $25 = $1,250,000) and held these stock in treasury.
Journal Entry:
-------------------
dr. Treasury Stock              $1,250,000
    cr. Cash                               $1,250,000
  • Treasury stock is NOT an asset

Reissue of TS

  • The company does not report a gain or loss on the reissue because the GAAP does not allow for income loss or gains from investments in its own stock because transactions with the owners are not consider profit-making 
    If National Beverage reissues 5,000 shares of this treasury stock for $28 per share (5,000 × $28 = $140,000), the accounting equation effects and journal entry would be:
Journal Entry:
-------------------
dr. Cash                        $140,000
    cr. Treasury Stock                   $125,000
    cr. Additional Pain in Capital       $15,000
    
If treasury stock were reissued at a price below its repurchase price, the difference between the repurchase price ($25 per share) and the reissue price (say, $23 per share) is recorded as a reduction in Additional Paid-in Capital. The accounting equation effects and journal entry for reissuing at an amount less than its cost are:
Journal Entry:
-------------------
dr. Cash                        $115,000
dr. Additional Pain in Capital  $10,000
    cr. Treasury Stock                   $115,000

Divideneds

  • Declared by the board of directors
  • Not legally required
  • Creates liability at declaration
  • Requires sufficient Retained Earning and Cash if you are issuing cash divideneds
  • Sometimes companies will put restrictions on how much dividened you can give out
  • Dates:
    • Declaration date:
      • Date the board of directors decides to give out dividened
      • Journal entry is made on this day
    • Date of Record:
      • No Journal entry
      • Only stockholders at this time will get a payment
    • Date of payment:
      • Decrease current assets (cash)
      • Decrease current liabilities
    • End of year:
      • Closing out dividened account
      • Retained earnings decreases due to pulling divideneds out of it
  • Only give dividened on outstanding shares 
  • dr. Dividends declared           xxx
    cr. Divideneds Pay              xxx
