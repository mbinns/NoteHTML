Module 1

Performance

  • What is job performance:
    • Employee behavior that contribute, both positively or negatively to the organization goal
    • These are typically behaviors under the control of the employees
    • Good Performer:
      • Task Performance
      • Counterproductive Behaviors:
        • Employee Behaviors that hinder organization goal accomplishment
        • Property Deviance:
          • Behaviors that harm the organization assets
          • Sabotage:
            • Purposeful destruction of equipment
            • Correction - Don't hire these people/fire them
          • Theft:
            • Stealing companies assets
            • Correction - Don't hire these people/fire them
        • Production deviance:
          • Behaviors that intentionally reduces the efficiency of work output
          • Wasting Resources:
            • People who do not do their jobs
            • Under performers:
              • Working to slowly
          • Substance abuse:
            • Abuse of drugs or alcohol before coming to work or while working
        • Political deviance:
          • Behaviors that intentionally reduce other individuals rather than the company as a whole
          • Gossip:
            • Conversations about others when you cannot prove the facts
          • Incivility:
            • Communication that is rude, impolite etc.
        • Personal aggression:
          • Hostile verbal and physical actions directed towards other employees
          • Harassment:
            • Occurs when employees are subject to unwanted verbal remarks and physical actions
          • Abuse:
            • Occurs when an employee is assaulted or endangers in such a way that injury either physical or psychological may occur
        • Typically an employee who engages in one form of CB will engage in multiple
        • How much CB should be put up with?:
          • Very little should be put up with

Job Analysis

  • Job performance should be based on job analysis
    • Organizations identify task performance behaviors by performing job analysis
  • Job Analysis Consists of:
    • A list of all the activities involved in a job:
      • Lists can be seen in a performance review form
    • Each activity on the list is rated by "subject matter experts" according to things like importance and frequency of the activity
      • Activities that are rated highly terms of their importance and frequency are used to define task performance

360 Feedback

  • These are meant to highlight the differences and the similarities on how people rank their abilities
  • 360 Feedback approach is the collection of performance information from anyone with firsthand knowledge about and employee:
    • Managers
    • Co-workers
    • The employee themselves
    • Etc.
  • This method is best suited to improving or developing employee talent
  • People typically lie on 360's
    • This is due to the social desirability bias because they want to be nice and liked by the person they are reviewing

Citizenship Behavior

  • Voluntary employee activity that may or may not be rewarded but it improves the quality of the work place
  • Interpersonal:
    • Helping:
      • Involves assisting coworkers who have heavy workloads, etc.
    • Courtesy:
      • Refers to keeping coworkers informed about matters that are relevant to them
    • Sportsmanship:
      • Involves maintaining a good attitude with coworkers
  • Organizational:
    • Voice:
      • Speaking up and offering constructive suggestions
    • Civic virtue:
      • Participating in company operations at a deeper than normal level
    • Boosterism:
      • Representing the organization in a positive way out in public

Motivation

  • Motivation is a set of energetic forces
    • These are both internal and external to a person
  • Initiates work-related effort
  • Determines its direction and persistence
  • Job performance is a combination of two factors:
    • Motivation
    • Ability
  • Grandmother's Rule:
    • Makes you decide to direct your effort to work assignments rather than take a break
  • Intrinsic Motivation:
    • Motivation that comes from yourself
  • Extrinsic Motivation:
    • Motivation that comes from others
  • Effect on Motivation:
    • Self-efficacy/competence (Highest)
      • People who feel a sense of internal self-confidence tend to outperform those who doubt their capabilities
    • Difficult goals (Second Highest):
      • Second most powerful motivating force
    • High levels of (Third):
      • Valence - Value of outcomes
      • Instrumentality - Outcome is related to my performance
      • Expectancy - More effort = Better Performance
    • Equity (Fourth)

Expectancy

  • Expectancy Theory:
    • Employee behavior is directed towards pleasure and not pain
    • Approach:
      • Avoidance
  • Shaped by:
    • Self-efficacy:
      • The belief that a person has the capabilities to execute the behaviors required for task success
    • Verbal persuasion:
      • Supportive comments from people
    • Emotional Cues:
      • Feelings of fear or anxiety can decrease expectancy
      • Feelings of pride can boost performance

Psychological Empowerment

  • Work tasks contribute to some larger purpose
  • Meaningfulness:
    • Work relates to a persons own ideals
  • Self-determination:
    • A Sense of choice in work tasks
  • Competence:
    • Capability to perform work tasks successfully
  • Impact:
    • A persons actions "Make a difference"

Compensation Plans

  • Individual Focused:
    • Piece Rate:
      • A specified rate is paid for each unit produced or sold
    • Merit Pay:
      • Increase in base salary is made in accordance with performance
      • Comes from evaluation ratings
    • Lump Sum Bonuses:
      • At risk pay that must be re-earned each year
      • Does not increase base salary
    • Recognition Awards:
      • Tangible awards:
        • Gift cards
        • Merchandise
        • Trips
        • Etc.
  • Unit Focused:
    • Gainsharing:
      • A bonus is received for meeting unit goals
      • The goal criteria are controllable by employees
  • Organization Focused:
    • Profit Sharing:
      • Bonus received when the publicly reported earnings of a company exceed some minimum level.
      • At risk pay that must be re-earned each year

Goal Setting Theory

  • Assigning employees specific and difficult goals will result in higher levels of performance
  • SMART:
    • Specific
    • Measurable
    • Achievable
    • Relevant
    • Time Bound - Deadlines)
  • Moderators on task performance
    • Feedback - Goals are more effective when people are provided with information about their progress in meeting the goals
    • Task Complexity - The effect of specific and difficult goals are twice as effective for simple goals
    • Goal Commitment - Performance is influenced by the extent to which a person accepts the goal

RSA Drive

  • What motivates us to do what we do
  • Study at MIT:
    • Gave students challenges and incentives for doing well on the tests
    • Results:
      • As long as the task was purely mechanical the better the performance, the better the reward:
        • High motivation
      • But once the task called for just a little bit of cognitive skill rewards stopped working:
        • Low motivation
  • Engagement:
    • Self direction is better for employee engagement
  • Mastery:
    • Urge to get better at stuff
    • Cos its fun is a good enough excuse for most people
  • Purpose motive:
    • Profit motive becomes unmarred from the purpose motive bad things happen:
      • Purpose and profit need to be tied together

Job Satisfaction

  • Pleasurable emotional state resulting from appraisal of ones job related experiences:
    • Represents how you feel about your job
  • Pay satisfaction:
    • Refers to an employees feelings about the companies promotional policies
    • Has moderate correlation with work satisfaction
  • Supervisor satisfaction:
    • Reflects employees feelings about their boss:
      • Can they help me attain the things that I value
      • Are they generally likable
    • Strong correlation with work satisfaction
  • Coworker satisfaction:
    • Refers to employees feelings about their fellow employees:
      • Can they help me do my job
      • Do I enjoy being around them
    • Strong correlation with work satisfaction
  • Satisfaction with the work itself:
    • Reflects employees feelings about their actual work tasks
    • Strong correlation with work satisfaction
  • Job Satisfaction is strongly related to Life Satisfaction or the degree to which employees feel a sense of happiness:
    • People feel better about their lives when they feel better about their jobs
    • Increases in job satisfaction have a stronger impact on life satisfaction than do increases in salary or income

Psychological States

  • Meaningfulness of work:
    • Reflects the degree to which work tasks are viewed as something that counts in the employees system of philosophies
  • Responsibility of outcomes:
    • Captures the degree to which employees feel that they are key drivers of the quality of the units work
  • Knowledge of results:
    • Reflects the extent to which employees know how well/poorly they are doing

Job Characteristics

  • Variety:
    • Degree to which the job requires different activities
  • Identity:
    • Is the degree to which the job requires completing a whole pace of work
  • Significance:
    • The degree to which the job has impact on the lives of other people
  • Autonomy:
    • Is the degree to which the job provides freedom to the individual doing work
  • Feedback:
    • IS the degree to which the individual knows how well they are performing

Ethics

  • Reflects the degree to which the behaviors of an authority are in accordance with generally accepted moral norms
  • Research on ethics seeks to explain why people behave in a manner consistent with generally accepted norms of morality and why people sometimes violate the norms
  • Morals:
    • Moral Awareness:
      • Occurs when an authority occurs when authority recognizes the moral issue exists
    • Moral Judgment:
      • When the authority accurately identifies the morally right course of action
    • Moral Intent:
      • Distinction between awareness or judgement because many unethical people know and understand that what they are doing is wrong
      • They just choose to do it anyways
    • Ethical Behavior:

Ahold Fraud

  • Fraud committed by over reporting supplier rebates
  • There are consequences to every unethical choice we make
  • unavoidable and certain
  • The person who committed the fraud is out of prison now and is an executive at a new company
  • The perpetrator:
    • Wasn't fooled or driven by greed
    • Falsified supplier rebate reports which left the fraudsters with more significant bonuses

Trust

  • Defined as the willingness to be vulnerable to an authority based on positive expectations
    • This correlates to the Willingness to take a risk
  • Trust=willingness to be vulnerable
  • Risk=actually becoming vulnerable

Disposition based

  • Means the your personality includes a general propensity to trust others
  • Factors:
    • Personality
  • New relationships fall here
  • Has less to do with the authority figure and more to do with the truster
    • Some trustors are high in trust propensity meaning that they have an expectation of individuals promises and words can be trusted
  • This is pretty high in the United States compared to other countries

Cognition based

  • Means trust is rooted in a rational assessment of authority
  • Factors:
    • Competence
    • Character
    • Benevolence
  • Most relationships fall here
  • Trustworthiness is defined as the characteristics or attributes of a trustee that inspire trust
    • Driven by a track record
  • Track Record
    • Competence:
      • Defined as skills and ability that enable the authority to be successful
    • Character:
      • Perception that the authority adheres to a set of values and principles the trustor finds acceptable
    • Benevolence:
      • Defined as the belief that that authority wants to do good for the trustor

Affect based trust

  • Depends on feelings toward the authority that go beyond rationality
  • Factors:
    • Feelings towards the person
  • Few relationships ever reach this level
  • Leap of faith:
    • As emotional bonds develops out feelings for the trustee further increase out willingness to accept vulnerability

Justice

  • Reflects the perceived fairness of an authority's decisions

Distributive

  • Reflects fairness of decision making outcomes
  • Employees gauge distributive justice by asking whether decision outcomes such as rewards evaluations promotions and work assignments are allocated using proper norms
  • Rules:
    • Equity vs Equality vs Need:
      • Are rewards allocated according to the proper norm

Procedural

  • Reflects the fairness of the decision making process
  • This is fostered when authorities adhere to rules
  • Rules:
    • Voice:
      • Concerns giving employees a chance to express their opinions
      • Improves employees acceptingness of decisions
    • Correctability:
      • Provides employees with a chance to request an appeal when the system has worked poorly
    • Consistency:
      • Help ensure everything is neutral across all parties
    • Bias suppression
      • Help ensure everything is neutral across all parties
    • Representativeness
      • Help ensure everything is neutral across all parties
  • When outcomes are bad procedural justice becomes VERY important
  • Procedural justice is a better driver of reactions to authorities

Interpersonal Justice

  • Reflects perceived fairness of treatment received by employees from authorities

Informational Justice

  • Reflects perceived fairness in the communications provided to employees from authorities about decisions
  • Radio shack fired 400 people with an 18 word email and then gave them 30 min to gtfo
  • Effects of good and bad communication:
    • Long sincere explanations for example with pay cuts halves the rate of employee theft
    • Short impersonal explanation

Module 2

Stress

  • A psychological response to demands when there is something at stake, and coping with the demands taxes/exceeds are persons ability or resources
    • The particular demands that cause people to feel stress are called stressors
    • Examples of negative consequences that occur when people are over stressed:
      • Headaches
      • Heart attacks
      • depression
      • violent behavior
  • Why are some employees more stressed than others:
    • Constitutional factors
    • Early childhood development
    • Skill level, competence
    • Ego Strength
    • Healthy Lifestyles
    • Coping Methods

Stressors

  • Work/Hindrance
    • Role conflict:
      • Refers to conflicting expectations that other people may have of us
    • Role ambiguity:
      • Refers to the lack of information regarding what needs to be done in a role
      • Also the unpredictability of consequences for not performing
    • Role Overload:
      • Occurs when the number of demanding roles a person holds is so high that the person simply cannot perform some or all effectively
    • Daily hassles:
      • Minor day to day demands that get in the way of accomplishing the things that we want to accomplish:
        • Unnecessary paperwork
        • Useless communications
        • Bureaucratic
  • Work Challenge:
    • Time pressure:
      • Strong sense that the time you have is not enough to complete the work
    • Work Complexity:
      • Refers to the degree to which the requirements of the work tax or exceed the abilities of the person
    • Work responsibility:
      • Refers to the nature of obligations that a person has to others
  • Non-work/Hindrance:
    • Work-Family Conflict:
      • Demands at work or home hinder fulfilment of the demands in the other role
    • Negative life events:
      • Hinder ability to achieve life goals
  • Non-work/Challenge:
    • Family time demands:
      • Reflect time that a person commits to participate in an array of family activities
    • Personal development:
      • Participation in formal education programs, golf lessons, etc
    • Positive life events:
      • Marriage, children, grand children

General Adaptation Syndrome

  • Suggests that the body has a set of responses that allow it to adapt and function in the face of stress
  • Yerkes Dodson law suggests that there is an optimal stress level where performance is at its best

Stage 1 Alarm

  • Resistance to the stressors is temporarily lowered
  • Stressors sinks in
  • Body starts to resist and counteract the stressor

Stage 2 Resistance

  • Increased arousal of the mind and body helps the person respond and adapt to the demand

Stage 3 Exhaustion

  • Prolonged or repasted exposure to the stressor could cause body to break down:
    • Possibly causing illness or even death

Coping Strategies

  • Different coping strategies address different demands
  • People pick the strategy based on some degree of control they feel it gives them over the problem
  • Behavior:
    • Working harder
    • Seeking assistance
    • Getting more resources
    • Doing something else for a while
    • Seeking support
    • Venting Anger
  • Cognitive:
    • Strategizing
    • Self-motivation
    • Changing priorities
    • Avoiding the problem
    • Looking for the positive in the negative
    • Reappraising

Stress Management

    • Training interventions:
      • Aimed at increasing job related competencies and skills
    • Supportive practices:
      • Flex time
      • Telecommuting
      • Compressed work week
  • Reduce Strains:
    • Health and wellness programs
  • What can you control:
    • Do something each day that gives you a sense of accomplishment:
      • Exercise
      • Learn
      • Read
    • Something you can say "At least I did that today"

Learned Helplessness

  • When lab animals were randomly shocked they learned to be helpless:
    • Because they thought they had no control over being shocked they stopped trying to avoid it
  • Could not unlearn the helpless behavior
  • Animals that could control the shocks were okay
  • Created model of clinical depression

  12. Every problem should have an ideal solution, and it is intolerable when one cannot be found

Big 5 Traits

Conscientiousness

  • Conscientiousness has the biggest influence on job performance
  • Conscientious employees prioritize accomplishment striving
  • Traits
    • Dependable
    • Organized
    • Reliable
    • Hardworking
    • Persevering

Agreeableness

  • Prioritize communion striving:
    • Strong desire to obtain acceptance in personal relationships
    • Beneficial in some situation and detrimental in others
  • Focus on getting along, not necessarily getting ahead
  • Traits:
    • Warm
    • Kind
    • Cooperative
    • Sympathetic
    • Helpful
    • Courteous

Extraversion

  • Easiest to judge in zero acquaintance situations (Situations where both parties are strangers)
  • Prioritize status striving:
    • Strong desire to obtain power and influence within a social structure as a means of expressing personality
  • Tend to be high in positive affectivity
    • Dispositional tendency to experience pleasant and engaging moods
  • Traits
    • Talkative
    • Sociable
    • Passionate
    • Assertive
    • Bold
    • Dominant

Neuroticism

  • Synonymous with negative affectivity
    • A dispositional tendency to experience unpleasant moods
  • Associated with differential exposure
    • More likely to appraise day to day situations as stressful
  • Associate with differential reactivity
    • Less likely to believe they can cope with stress
  • Locus of control is typically external meaning they don't think they can influence events that effect them
  • Less neurotic people typically have more of an internal locus of control
  • Traits
    • Nervous
    • Moody
    • Emotional
    • Insecure
    • Jealous

Openness to experience

  • Also called inquisitiveness, Intellectualness, or even Culture
  • More likely to be valuable in jobs that require high levels of creativity
  • Highly open individuals are more likely to migrate into artistic and scientific fields
  • Traits:
    • Curious
    • Imaginative
    • Creative
    • Complex
    • Refined
    • Sophisticated

Myers-Briggs

  • MBTI evaluates individuals on basis of four types of preferences
  • Extraversion:
    • Being energized by people and social interactions
    • How extroverted are you compared to your levels of inter version
  • Sensing:
    • Proffering clear and concrete facts and data
    • Compare to how much intuition which is how much you like to infer data
  • Thinking:
    • Approaches decisions with logic and critical analysis
    • Compare to how much stock is put into "feeling" when making a decision (I.E. how much social influence is present in the decision making process)
  • Judging:
    • Approaches tasks by planning and goal setting
    • Compare to Perceiving (I.E. Winging it)

DISC

  • Dominance:
    • Relating to control power and assertiveness
  • Influence:
    • Social situations and communication
  • Steadiness:
    • Patience, persistence and thoughtfulness
  • Conscientiousness:
    • Structure and organization

RIASEC

  • Realistic:
    • Enjoy practical hands on tasks
  • Investigative:
    • Abstract analytical theory oriented tasks
  • Artistic:
    • Enjoy entertaining and fascinating other using imagination
  • Social:
    • Enjoy helping,serving or assisting others
  • Enterprising:
    • Enjoy persuading leading or outperforming others
  • Conventional:
    • Enjoy organizing counting or regulating people or things

BFOQ

  • Bona Fide Occupational Qualification
  • A necessary (not just proffered) qualification for performing a job
  • Supreme Court has ruled that BFOQ's are limited to policies directly related to a workers ability to do the job

Tests

  • Integrity Tests:
    • Focus specifically on predisposition to engage in theft and other counterproductive behaviors
    • More strongly related to job performance than conscientiousness tests
  • Clear purpose tests:
    • Asks applicants about their attitudes toward
      • Dishonesty
      • Beliefs about how frequent dishonesty
      • Endorsement of common rationalizations for dishonesty
      • Desire to punish dishonesty
    • Example:
      • Did you ever think about taking money from where you worked but didn't go through with it?
      • Is it OK to get around the law if you don't break it
      • If you were sent an extra item with an order would you send it back?
  • Veiled purpose tests:
    • Assess more general personality traits that are associated with dishonest acts
    • Example:
      • I like to plan things ahead
      • I've never hurt anyones feelings
      • I don't feel I have control over my life
  • About 1/3 of fortune 1000 companies plan to implement some form of personality testing
  • Almost everyone fakes on personality tests (Lies, or exaggerates on questions)
    • Since Almost everyone fakes to some degree correlations with outcomes like theft or other counterproductive behaviors are relatively unaffected

Workplace Violence

  • For women the number 1 cause of death in the work place is homicide
  • Form men the number 2 cause of death in the work place is homicide
  • Two types of violence:
    • First is where there are threats and intend to carry them out
    • Second is where there are threats and no intent to carry them out

Learning and Decision Making

  • Learning:
    • Reflects relatively permanent changes in an employees knowledge or skill that results from experience
      • The more an employee learns the more value they can bring to work
    • Decision Making:
      • Refers to the process of generating and choosing from a set of alternatives to solve a problem
    • Expertise:
      • Refers to the knowledge and skills that distinguish experts from novices

Types of Knowledge

  • Explicit Knowledge:
    • is the kind of information you are likely to think about when you picture someone sitting down at a desk to learn
    • Characteristics
      • Easily Transferred through written or verbal communication
      • Readily available
      • Can be learned through books
      • General info
  • Tacit Knowledge:
    • What people can learn through experience
    • 90% of knowledge in an org is tacit
    • Characteristics
      • Very difficult if not impossible to explain to others
      • Highly personal in nature
      • Based on experience
      • Sometimes people don't even know they have the skill
      • Typically job and situation specific

Methods of Learning

  • We learn through both positive and negative reinforcement, observation, and experience
  • Operant conditioning:
    • Says we learn by observing the link between our actions and the results that follow

Observation

  • Social Learning theory:
    • Suggests that people in organizations have the ability to learn through the observations of others
  • Behavioral modeling:
    • Happens when employees observe the actions of others and learn from what they saw and repeat the behavior
    • Process:
      • Attentional:
        • Learner focuses attention on the critical behaviors exhibited by the model
      • Retention:
        • Learner must remember the behaviors of the model once the model has been removed
      • Production:
        • Learner must have the appropriate skill set to reproduce the behavior
      • Reinforcement:
        • Must view the model receiving reinforcements for the behavior and then also get the same ones

Reinforcement

  • Contingencies of Reinforcement:
    • Positive reinforcement:
      • Positive outcome follows a behavior
    • Negative reinforcement:
      • Unwanted outcome is removed following a behavior
      • Perform task to not get yelled at
    • Punishment:
      • Unwanted outcome follows a behavior
    • Extinction:
      • Removal of consequences following an unwanted behavior
    • Positive reinforcement and Extinction should be the most common forms of reinforcement used by managers
  • Schedules:
    • Continuous:
      • Simplest and happens when a specific consequence follows each behavior
      • Potential level of performance is High
      • Example:
        • Praise
    • Intermittent:
      • Happens when a consequence does not follow after each behavior
    • Fixed:
      • Workers rewarded after a certain amount of time and the length between stays the same
      • Potential level of performance is average
      • Example:
        • Paycheck
    • Variable interval:
      • Designed to reinforce behavior at more random points in time
      • Potential level of performance is Moderately high
      • Example:
        • Supervisor comes by
    • Fixed ratio schedules:
      • Consequences follow after a certain number of behavior(s)
      • Potential level of performance is High
      • Example:
        • Piece-rate pay
    • Variable ratio:
      • Reward based on varying number of exhibited behaviors
      • Potential level of performance is Very High
      • Example:
        • Commission pay

Goal Orientation

  • Learning orientation:
    • Learning for its own sake
    • Enjoy working on new tasks even if they fail early in the process
  • Performance orientation:
    • Focuses on demonstrating competence so that others think favorably of them

Methods of Decision Making

  • Programmed decisions:
    • Somewhat automatic
    • Persons knowledge allows him or her to recognize and ID a situation
    • Intuition:
      • Emotionally charged judgements that arise through quick non-conscious and holistic associations
  • Non-Programmed:
    • Situation arises that is new, complex and not recognized:
      • Employee moving up the corporate ladder a larger percentage of their decisions become non-programmed
  • Rational:
    • Step by step approach to making decisions that max outcomes by examining all available alternatives
    • Identify the problem thoroughly and examine situation and all interested parties
    • Develop an exhaustive list of alternatives
    • Evaluate all alternatives simultaneously
    • Pick the best

Problems in decision making

  • Bounded rationality:
    • Decision makers do not have the ability or resources to process all available info
    • Boil it down to something easily understood
    • Come up with a few solutions that ten to be straightforward, familiar, and similar to what is currently being done
    • Evaluate each as soon as it is thought of
    • Use distorted and inaccurate info during eval process
    • Pick the first acceptable solution (Satisficing)
  • Selective perception:
    • People see problems only as they affect them and if it is consistent with their expectations
  • Heuristics:
    • Simple efficient rule of thumb that allows us to make decisions more easily
  • When in doubt:
    • Gather more data
    • To every problem there is a simple solution that wont work
    • Latencies are diagnostic
    • Pathology is comfortable
    • Irrational behavior always makes sense
    • Nature hates vacuum
  • Social Identity Theory:
    • People identify themselves by the groups to which they belong and perceive and judge others by their group memberships
  • Stereotype:
    • assumptions made about somebody based on their group membership
  • Fundamental attribution error:
    • People blame others failures on internal factors
  • Self-serving:
    • People blame their own failures on external factors
  • Escalation of commitment:
    • Following through a failing course of action
  • Best predictor of future behavior is past behavior
  • You can't change what you can't/don't measure
  • Have your meeting before your meeting

Biases

  • Anchoring:
    • Tendency to rely too heavily on one trait or piece of info when making decisions
  • Availably Bias:
    • Biased prediction due to the tendency to focus on the most salient and emotionally charged outcome
  • Bandwagon effect:
    • The tendency to do/believe things because many others do
  • Choice supportive:
    • Tendency to remember ones choices as better than they actually were
  • Confirmation:
    • Tendency to search for information that lines up with your opinions
  • Contrast effect:
    • Enhancement or diminish mend of a weight or other measurement when compared with recently observed contrasting object
  • False consensus:
    • Tendency for people to overestimate the degree to which others agree with them
  • Gamblers fallacy:
    • Tendency to assume that individual random events are influenced by previous random events
  • Halo:
    • Tendency for a persons positive or negative traits to spill over from one area of their personality into others perceptions of their traits
    • Example:
      • Someone is very nice so people also think they can do good work
  • Hindsight:
    • Sometimes called the knew it all along effect, seeing past events as predictable
  • Illusion of control:
    • Tendency for humans to believe they can control or at least influence events they clearly cant
  • Primacy effect:
    • Tendency to weigh initial events more than subsequent events
  • Projection:
    • Tendency to unconsciously assume others share same opinions
  • Recency:
    • Weight recent events greater than past events
  • Self fulfilling prophecy:
    • Tendency to engage in behaviors that result in the confirmation of out beliefs

Teams

  • Consists of two or more people who work interdependently over some time period
  • Teams differ from groups in two primary respects:
    • Interactions within teams revolve around a deeper dependence on one another than groups
    • Interactions within teams occur with specific task-related purposes in mind

Characteristics

  • Team characteristics are comprised of
    • Team type
    • Team interdependence type
    • Team composition
  • Team Effectiveness:
    • Performance:
      • Ability to produce a good or service
      • Quality of good or service
      • Quantity of good or service
    • Team viability:
      • Refers to the likelihood that the team can work together effectively into the future
  • Task interdependence and Team performance relationship is moderately positive:
    • Correlation is higher in teams involved in more complex knowledge work rather than less complex physical work
  • Task interdependence and Team commitment is weaker
    • Correlation is higher in teams involved in more complex knowledge work rather than less complex clerical work

Types

  • Work:
    • Long Life span
    • Produce goods/services
    • High involvement
  • Management:
    • Long Life span
    • Integrate activities of sub-units across business functions
    • Moderate involvement
  • Parallel
    • Variable Life span
    • Provide recommendations and resolve issues
    • Low involvement
  • Project:
    • Short Life span
    • Produce one-time product or service
    • Variable involvement
  • Action:
    • Short Life span
    • Performs complex tasks that are short in duration and take place in highly challenging or visible environments
    • Variable involvement
  • Virtual:
    • Teams are separated by barriers forcing them to work over electronic communication

Stages of Development

  • Forming:
    • Try to understand boundaries and get a feel for what is expected
    • Punctuated equilibrium:
      • Members make assumptions and establish behavior that lasts for the first half of the teams life
  • Storming:
    • Remain committed to own ideas, triggering conflicts that affect some relationships and harms teams progress
  • Norming:
    • Realize they need to work together
  • Performing:
    • Members are comfortable and progress is made

Interdependence

  • Task
    • refers to the degree to which teams interact with and rely on other team members for information to accomplish work
  • Pooled
    • requires lowest degree of coordination. Members complete their work independently and then the work is piled up to represent the total team output
  • Sequential
    • Different tasks must be done in a prescribed order
    • Interaction only occurs with people whose tasks are right next to one another in the timeline
  • Reciprocal:
    • Members are specialized to perform specific tasks
    • Members work with whom ever they'd like
  • Comprehensive:
    • Requires the highest level of interaction and coordination among members
    • Each member has a great deal of discretion in terms of what they do and with whom
  • Goal:
    • Members have a shared vision of the teams goal and align their individual goals with that vision
    • Formalized mission statement
  • Outcome:
    • Exists when members share in the rewards of the outcome
    • Obvious compensation connection practices in organizations
    • High interdependence here promotes higher levels of cooperation
    • Hybrid:
      • Rewards are based on individual AND team performance

Composition

  • Mix of people who make up the team
  • Member Role:
    • Leader:
      • Makes decisions for the team and provides direction and control over members who perform assigned tasks
    • Team task roles:
      • Refer to behaviors that directly facilitate the accomplishment of the team task
    • Team building roles:
      • Behaviors that influence social climate
    • Individualistic:
      • Reflect behaviors that benefit the individual at the expense of the team
  • Member Ability
  • Member Personality:
    • Personalities play a role in how a team functions
  • Member Diversity:
    • Degree to which members are different from one another
    • Valuable in problem solving due to many different outlooks
    • Similarity attraction
      • People are more attracted to people who are similar to them
    • Surface Level:
      • Differences based on observable traits:
        • Sex
        • Age
        • Race
      • These go away
    • Deep level:
      • Diversity with respect to attributes that are not easy to observe but can be inferred after long exposure
      • Get worse
  • Size:
    • Greater number of members is good for management and project teams but not for teams trying to make something
    • Teams of 4-5 are the best size
    • Two Pizza Rule

Processes

  • Term reflects different activities and interactions occurring within teams
  • Team characteristics affect team processes
  • Have strong impact on effectiveness

Value

  • Process Gain:
    • Getting more from the team than you would expect according to the capabilities of individuals
  • Process loss:
    • Getting less from the team than you would expect according to the capabilities of individuals
    • Coordination loss:
      • Consumes time and energy that could otherwise be devoted to task
      • Production blocking:
        • Occurs when members have to wait on one another to move forward with their part
    • Motivational loss:
      • Team members not working as hards as they should
    • Social loafing:
      • Team member exert less effort when working in teams than individually
  • Taskwork:
    • Are activities of team members relating directly to accomplishment of tasks
    • Creative behavior:
      • Brainstorming:
        • Each member offers as many potential solutions as possible to a problem
      • Nominal:
        • Written brainstorming designed to reduce social loafing
  • Boundary spanning:
    • Involves activities with individuals and groups other than those who are considered part of the team
    • Ambassador activities:
      • Refer to communication designed to protect the team or acquire favor/resources for the team
    • Task coordinator activities:
      • Involve communications that are intended to coordinate task related issues
    • Scout:
      • Refer to things team members do to obtain info about the broader marketplace
  • Transition:
    • Focus of prep for future work
  • Action:
    • Monitoring progress toward goals and coordinating things as they fall into place
  • Interpersonal:
    • Conflict management:
      • Find intention because people are rarely douche bags

States

  • Refer to specific thought and feelings about their experience working together
  • Cohesion:
    • Members develop strong bonds to the team and the members of the team
    • Groupthink:
      • Happens in highly cohesive teams due to wanting to maintain harmony
    • Appoint a devils advocate to challenge the group opinion
  • Potency:
    • Refers to the degree in which members believe the team is effective in a variety of tasks
    • High potency members are confident that their team can perform well:
      • They focus more energy into the task
    • Team members confidence in their own abilities play a role in high potency
  • Mental Models:
    • Level of common understanding among team members with regard to important aspects of the team and tasks
  • Transactive memory:
    • How specialized knowledge is distributed among members in a manner that results in an effective team

Leadership and Power

Organizational Politics

  • Can be seen as actions by individuals that are directed towards the goal of furthering own self interests
  • Political skill:
    • Is the ability to effectively understand others at work and use that knowledge to influence others
    • Networking ability
    • Social astuteness, tendency to observe others and accurately interpret their behavior

Influence

  • Is the use of an actual behavior that causes behavioral or attitudinal changes in others
  • Influence can be seen as directional:
    • Downward Managers -> Employees:
      • Most frequent
    • Lateral Peers -> Peers
    • Upward Employees -> Managers
  • Influences is all relative:
    • Absolute power of influencer and influence

Influence Tactics

  • Influence tactics tend to be most successful when used in combination
  • The influence tactics that tend to be most successful
  • Rational persuasion:
    • Is the use of logical arguments and hard facts to show the target that the request is a worthwhile one
  • Inspiration appeal:
    • Is a tactic designed to appeal to the targets values and ideals thereby creating emotional or attitudinal reaction
  • Consultation:
    • Occurs when the target is allowed to participate in deciding how to carry out or implement a request
  • Collaboration:
    • A leader uses collaboration by attempting to make it easier for the target to complete the request
  • Ingratiation:
    • Use of favors complements or friendly behavior to make the target feel better about the influencer
  • Personal appeals:
    • Are when the requester asks for something based on personal friendship or loyalty
  • Exchange tactic:
    • Is used when the requester offers a reward or resource to the target in return for performing
  • Apprising occurs when the requester clearly explains why performing the request will benefit the target personally
  • Pressure:
    • Use of coercive power through threats and demands
  • Coalitions
    • Occur when the influencer enlists others to help influence a target

Leadership

  • Use of power and influence to direct the activities of follower towards a goal

Time-Driven

  • Model suggests that focus should shift from autocratic, facilitative, delegative leaders to autocratic, facilitative, delegative situations
  • Decision Significance:
    • Is the decision significant to the success of the project organization
  • Importance of commitment:
    • Is it important that employees buy in to the decision
  • Leader expertise:
    • Does the leader have significant knowledge or expertise regarding the problem
  • Likelihood of commitment:
    • How likely is it that employees will trust the leader decision and commit to it
  • Shared Objectives:
    • Do employees share and support the same objects, or do they have an agenda of their own
  • Employee expertise:
    • Do the employees have significant knowledge or expertise regarding the problem
  • Teamwork skills Do the employees have the ability to work together to solve the problem, or will they struggle with conflicts

Day-to-Day Behaviors

  • Initiating Structure:
    • Reflects the extent to which leader defines and structures the roles of employees in pursuit of goal attainment
    • Leaders who are high on initiating structure play a more active role in directing group activities and prioritize planning scheduling and trying out new ideas
    • Initiation:
      • Originating, facilitating, and sometimes resisting new ideas and practices
    • Organization:
      • Define and structuring work, clarifying leader versus member roles, coordinating employee tasks
    • Production:
      • Setting goals and providing incentives for the effort and productive of employees
  • Consideration:
    • Reflects the extent to which leaders create job relationship characterized by mutual trust respect for employees ideas and consideration of employee feelings:
      • Leaders who are high on consideration create a climate of good rapport and strong two way communication
    • Membership:
      • Mixing with Employees, stressing informal interactions, and exchanging personal services
    • Integration:
      • Encouraging a pleasant atmosphere, reducing conflict, promoting individual adjustment to the group
    • Communication:
      • Providing information to employees seeking information from them, showing awareness of matters that affect them
    • Recognition:
      • Expressing approval or disapproval of the behaviors of employees
    • Representation:
      • Acting on behalf of the group, defending the group and advancing the interests of the group

Readiness

  • Optimal combination of initiating structure and consideration depends on the readiness of the employees in the work unit
  • Readiness is broadly defined as the degree to which employees have the ability and willingness to accomplish their specific tasks:
    • R1 - unable and unwilling
      • Telling:
    • R2 - unable but willing
      • Selling:
    • R3 - able but unwilling
      • Participating:
    • R4 - able and willing
      • Delegating:

Life Cycle Theory

  • Telling:
    • High initiating structure, Low consideration
    • The leader provides specific instructions and closely supervises performance
  • Selling:
    • High initiating structure, High consideration
    • The leader explains key issues and provides opportunities for clarification
  • Participating:
    • Low initiating structure, High consideration
    • The leader shares ideas and tries to help the group conduct its affairs
  • Delegating:
    • Low initiating structure, Low consideration
    • The leader turns responsibility for key behaviors over to the employees

Effectiveness

  • Leader effectiveness:
    • Is the degree to which the leaders actions result in the achievement of the units goals. The continued commitment of the employees and development of trust, respect and obligation in leader member dyads
  • Traits are more predictive in leader emergence, I.E. who becomes a leader then leader effectiveness:
    • High conscientiousness:
      • Linked to Emergence
    • Low agreeableness:
      • Linked to Emergence
    • Low neuroticism:
      • Linked to Neither
    • High openness to experience:
      • Linked to both emergence and effectiveness
    • High extraversion
      • Linked to both emergence and effectiveness
    • High general cognitive ability
      • Linked to both emergence and effectiveness
    • High energy level
      • Linked to both emergence and effectiveness
    • High stress tolerance
      • Linked to both emergence and effectiveness
    • High self-confidence
      • Linked to both emergence and effectiveness

Power

  • Ability to influence the behavior of others and resist unwanted influence
  • Just because a person has the ability to influence others does not mean they will chose to do so
  • Power can be seen as the ability to resist the influence attempts of others

Types

  • Organizational:
    • Legitimate power:
      • Derived form a position of authority inside the org and is sometimes reared to as formal authority
      • Polite clear requests
      • Explain reasoning of requests
      • Don't exceed your scope of authority
      • Follow up on requests
      • Insist on compliance if appropriate
    • Reward power:
      • Exists when someone has control over the resources or rewards another person wants
      • Offer what people want
      • Offer fair and ethical rewards
      • Don't over promise
      • Explain criteria for giving rewards and keep it simple
      • Don't fail to deliver
      • Don't use rewards to manipulate
    • Coercive power:
      • Exists when they control punishments
      • Explain rules and requirements to ensure people understand the consequences
      • Respond to infractions promptly with no favoritism
      • Get facts before following through
      • Provide warnings
      • Fair punishments that equal actions
  • Personal:
    • Expert:
      • Derived from a persons expertise, skill, or knowledge
      • Explain reasons for requests and why its important
      • Provide evidence that a proposal will be successful
      • Don't make rash/inconstant statements
      • Don't exaggerate
      • Listen seriously to persons concerns and suggestions
      • Act confluently in a crisis
    • Referent:
      • Exists when others have a desire to associate and identify with the person
      • Show support
      • Be sincere forms of integrations
      • Defend and back up when appropriate
      • Make self-sacrifices to show concern
      • Keep promises

Contingencies

  • Substitutability:
    • There are no substitutes for the rewards or resources the leader controls
  • Centrality:
    • The leaders role is important and interdependent with others in the org
  • Discretion:
    • The leader has the freedom to make their own decisions without being restrained by org rules
  • Visibility:
    • Others know about the leader and their resources

Decision Making

Styles

  • Captures how a leader decides as opposed to what they do
  • Autocratic:
    • Makes decisions alone
    • Highest Leadership control
  • Consultative:
    • Presents the problem to the group and asks for suggestions, ultimately making the decision themselves
    • Second highest leadership control
  • Facilitative:
    • Presents the problem to the group and seeks for the group to come to a consensus
    • Second highest follower control
  • Delegative:
    • Leader gives individuals or groups the responsibility for making decisions with a set of rules
    • Highest follower control
  • Transformational:
    • Inspiring followers to commit to a shared vision that provides meaning to their work while also serving as a role model:
      • Idealized influence:
        • Involves behaving in ways that earn the admiration, trust, and respect of followers, causing followers to want to identify with and emulate the leader
        • Leader instills pride in me for being associated with him/her
      • Inspirational motivation:
        • Involves behaving in ways that foster an enthusiasm for and commitment to a shared vision
        • Articulates a compelling vision of the future
      • Intellectual stimulation:
        • Involves behaving in ways that challenge followers to be more innovative and creative by questioning assumptions and reframing old situations in new ways
        • Leader gets others to look at problems from many different angles
      • Individualized consideration:
        • Involves behaving in ways that help followers achieve their potential through coaching development and mentoring
        • Leader spends a lot of time teaching and coaching
  • Laissez faire:
    • Hands off is the avoidance of leadership altogether
  • Transactional:
    • Occurs when the leader rewards or disciplines the follower depending on the adequacy of followers performance
    • Passive management by exception the leader waits around for mistakes and errors, then takes corrective action as necessary
    • Active management by exception the leader arranges to monitor mistakes and errors actively and again takes corrective action when required
    • Contingent reward happens when the leader attains follower agreement on what needs to be done using promised or actual rewards in exchange for adequate performance

Organizational Culture

  • System of shared norms, beliefs, values and assumptions which bind people together thereby creating shared meanings
  • The personality of an org:
    • Provides sense of identity to members
    • Helps legitimize the management system of the org
    • Clarifies and reinforces standards of behavior
  • Culture is social knowledge among members of the org
  • Culture defines:
    • Rules
    • Norms
    • Values
  • Culture shapes and reinforces certain employee attitudes and behaviors by creating a system of control

Symbols

  • Can be found throughout an org, from its corporate logo to the images it places on its web site to the uniforms people wear
  • Language reflects the jargon, slang and slogans used within the org:
    • Acronyms
    • Go4Zero

Basic Components

  • Three components:
    • Espoused Values:
      • Values the company declares they have
    • Basic underlying assumptions
      • These are taken for granted, meaning that beliefs and philosophies are so ingrained that employees don't even think about doing them they just act on them
    • Observable artifacts:
      • Things that can be seen or heard like languages and symbols

Pros and cons

  • Pro:
    • Differentiates the org from others
    • Allows employees to ID themselves with the org
    • Facilitates desired behaviors among employees
    • Creates stability within the org
  • Cons:
    • Makes merging with other organizations harder
    • Attracts and retains similar kinds of people
    • Can be too much of a good thing creating extreme behaviors among employees
    • Makes adapting to the environment harder

Attraction Selection Attrition

  • Holds that potential employees will be attracted to the org whose cultures match their own personality
  • Some potential job applicants wont apply due to a perceived lack of fit
  • Orgs will select candidates based on whether their personalities fit the culture
  • People who don't fit will be unhappy or ineffective

Realistic job preview

  • Process of ensuring that a potential employee understands the positive and negatives of the job:
    • One of the most inexpensive and effective ways of reducing early turnover
    • Occurs during the anticipatory stage of ASA and recruitment process

Socialization

  • The more quickly an employee is socialized the sooner they will become effective
    • New-comer orientation:
      • Session held for new hires to learn more about the org
      • Effective way to start the socialization process
      • Effective transmitters of socialization content:
        • Employees who complete orientation have higher levels of satisfaction, commitment and performance than those who don't

Sexual harassment

Statistics

  • Out of 782 workers:
    • 31% of women say they were sexually harassed
    • 7% of male workers say they were
    • 62% took no action
    • 43% were harassed by a supervisor

Definition

  • Form of discrimination and violates the Title VII act of the Civil Rights Act of 1964:
    • Because they treat individuals differently based on their sex
  • Secual harassment is defined as unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors and other verbal or physical conduct

EEOC Types

  • Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
  • Quid pro quo:
    • Person makes a benefit or punishment continent on employees submitting to the request
  • Hostile work env:
    • Behavior of a coworker creates and env that makes it difficult to say no to the request/ or work
  • Submission to such conduct is made either explicitly or implicitly a term or condition of an individuals employment
  • Submission or rejection of advances is used in employment decisions
  • Interferes with the employees work

Workplace Guidelines

  • Employers should establish a guideline on how to deal with cases of sexual harassment
  • No sexual relations with coworkers that you did not agree to
  • No inappropriate touching or jokes
  • Train employees on sexual harassment recognition and how to avoid the behavior
  • Provide a means for employees to complain and be protected

Effects

  • Unhappy home life
  • Decrease in confidence
  • Decrease job productivity
  • Depression
  • Drug use
  • Fatigue

Stopping harassment

  • Write down what happened
  • Tell HR
  • Report them
  • File discrimination report with the EEOC

Teen harassment

  • Low skilled workers are targeted because they can be replaced quickly if they refuse the advances
  • Teens are likely targets because they are low skilled workers with out many options
