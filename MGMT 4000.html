Module 1

Performance

  • What is job performance:
    • Employee behavior that contribute, both positively or negatively to the organization goal
    • These are typically behaviors under the control of the employees
    • Good Performer:
      • Task Performance
      • Counterproductive Behaviors:
        • Employee Behaviors that hinder organization goal accomplishment
        • Property Deviance:
          • Behaviors that harm the organization assets
          • Sabotage:
            • Purposeful destruction of equipment
            • Correction - Don't hire these people/fire them
          • Theft:
            • Stealing companies assets
            • Correction - Don't hire these people/fire them
        • Production deviance:
          • Behaviors that intentionally reduces the efficiency of work output
          • Wasting Resources:
            • People who do not do their jobs
            • Under performers:
              • Working to slowly
          • Substance abuse:
            • Abuse of drugs or alcohol before coming to work or while working
        • Political deviance:
          • Behaviors that intentionally reduce other individuals rather than the company as a whole
          • Gossip:
            • Conversations about others when you cannot prove the facts
          • Incivility:
            • Communication that is rude, impolite etc.
        • Personal aggression:
          • Hostile verbal and physical actions directed towards other employees
          • Harassment:
            • Occurs when employees are subject to unwanted verbal remarks and physical actions
          • Abuse:
            • Occurs when an employee is assaulted or endangers in such a way that injury either physical or psychological may occur
        • Typically an employee who engages in one form of CB will engage in multiple
        • How much CB should be put up with?:
          • Very little should be put up with

Job Analysis

  • Job performance should be based on job analysis
    • Organizations identify task performance behaviors by performing job analysis
  • Job Analysis Consists of:
    • A list of all the activities involved in a job:
      • Lists can be seen in a performance review form
    • Each activity on the list is rated by "subject matter experts" according to things like importance and frequency of the activity
      • Activities that are rated highly terms of their importance and frequency are used to define task performance

360 Feedback

  • These are meant to highlight the differences and the similarities on how people rank their abilities
  • 360 Feedback approach is the collection of performance information from anyone with firsthand knowledge about and employee:
    • Managers
    • Co-workers
    • The employee themselves
    • Etc.
  • This method is best suited to improving or developing employee talent
  • People typically lie on 360's
    • This is due to the social desirability bias because they want to be nice and liked by the person they are reviewing

Citizenship Behavior

  • Voluntary employee activity that may or may not be rewarded but it improves the quality of the work place
  • Interpersonal:
    • Helping:
      • Involves assisting coworkers who have heavy workloads, etc.
    • Courtesy:
      • Refers to keeping coworkers informed about matters that are relevant to them
    • Sportsmanship:
      • Involves maintaining a good attitude with coworkers
  • Organizational:
    • Voice:
      • Speaking up and offering constructive suggestions
    • Civic virtue:
      • Participating in company operations at a deeper than normal level
    • Boosterism:
      • Representing the organization in a positive way out in public

Motivation

  • Motivation is a set of energetic forces
    • These are both internal and external to a person
  • Initiates work-related effort
  • Determines its direction and persistence
  • Job performance is a combination of two factors:
    • Motivation
    • Ability
  • Grandmother's Rule:
    • Makes you decide to direct your effort to work assignments rather than take a break
  • Intrinsic Motivation:
    • Motivation that comes from yourself
  • Extrinsic Motivation:
    • Motivation that comes from others
  • Effect on Motivation:
    • Self-efficacy/competence (Highest)
      • People who feel a sense of internal self-confidence tend to outperform those who doubt their capabilities
    • Difficult goals (Second Highest):
      • Second most powerful motivating force
    • High levels of (Third):
      • Valence - Value of outcomes
      • Instrumentality - Outcome is related to my performance
      • Expectancy - More effort = Better Performance
    • Equity (Fourth)

Expectancy

  • Expectancy Theory:
    • Employee behavior is directed towards pleasure and not pain
    • Approach:
      • Avoidance
  • Shaped by:
    • Self-efficacy:
      • The belief that a person has the capabilities to execute the behaviors required for task success
    • Verbal persuasion:
      • Supportive comments from people
    • Emotional Cues:
      • Feelings of fear or anxiety can decrease expectancy
      • Feelings of pride can boost performance

Psychological Empowerment

  • Work tasks contribute to some larger purpose
  • Meaningfulness:
    • Work relates to a persons own ideals
  • Self-determination:
    • A Sense of choice in work tasks
  • Competence:
    • Capability to perform work tasks successfully
  • Impact:
    • A persons actions "Make a difference"

Compensation Plans

  • Individual Focused:
    • Piece Rate:
      • A specified rate is paid for each unit produced or sold
    • Merit Pay:
      • Increase in base salary is made in accordance with performance
      • Comes from evaluation ratings
    • Lump Sum Bonuses:
      • At risk pay that must be re-earned each year
      • Does not increase base salary
    • Recognition Awards:
      • Tangible awards:
        • Gift cards
        • Merchandise
        • Trips
        • Etc.
  • Unit Focused:
    • Gainsharing:
      • A bonus is received for meeting unit goals
      • The goal criteria are controllable by employees
  • Organization Focused:
    • Profit Sharing:
      • Bonus received when the publicly reported earnings of a company exceed some minimum level.
      • At risk pay that must be re-earned each year

Goal Setting Theory

  • Assigning employees specific and difficult goals will result in higher levels of performance
  • SMART:
    • Specific
    • Measurable
    • Achievable
    • Relevant
    • Time Bound - Deadlines)
  • Moderators on task performance
    • Feedback - Goals are more effective when people are provided with information about their progress in meeting the goals
    • Task Complexity - The effect of specific and difficult goals are twice as effective for simple goals
    • Goal Commitment - Performance is influenced by the extent to which a person accepts the goal

RSA Drive

  • What motivates us to do what we do
  • Study at MIT:
    • Gave students challenges and incentives for doing well on the tests
    • Results:
      • As long as the task was purely mechanical the better the performance, the better the reward:
        • High motivation
      • But once the task called for just a little bit of cognitive skill rewards stopped working:
        • Low motivation
  • Engagement:
    • Self direction is better for employee engagement
  • Mastery:
    • Urge to get better at stuff
    • Cos its fun is a good enough excuse for most people
  • Purpose motive:
    • Profit motive becomes unmarred from the purpose motive bad things happen:
      • Purpose and profit need to be tied together

Job Satisfaction

  • Pleasurable emotional state resulting from appraisal of ones job related experiences:
    • Represents how you feel about your job
  • Pay satisfaction:
    • Refers to an employees feelings about the companies promotional policies
    • Has moderate correlation with work satisfaction
  • Supervisor satisfaction:
    • Reflects employees feelings about their boss:
      • Can they help me attain the things that I value
      • Are they generally likable
    • Strong correlation with work satisfaction
  • Coworker satisfaction:
    • Refers to employees feelings about their fellow employees:
      • Can they help me do my job
      • Do I enjoy being around them
    • Strong correlation with work satisfaction
  • Satisfaction with the work itself:
    • Reflects employees feelings about their actual work tasks
    • Strong correlation with work satisfaction
  • Job Satisfaction is strongly related to Life Satisfaction or the degree to which employees feel a sense of happiness:
    • People feel better about their lives when they feel better about their jobs
    • Increases in job satisfaction have a stronger impact on life satisfaction than do increases in salary or income

Psychological States

  • Meaningfulness of work:
    • Reflects the degree to which work tasks are viewed as something that counts in the employees system of philosophies
  • Responsibility of outcomes:
    • Captures the degree to which employees feel that they are key drivers of the quality of the units work
  • Knowledge of results:
    • Reflects the extent to which employees know how well/poorly they are doing

Job Characteristics

  • Variety:
    • Degree to which the job requires different activities
  • Identity:
    • Is the degree to which the job requires completing a whole pace of work
  • Significance:
    • The degree to which the job has impact on the lives of other people
  • Autonomy:
    • Is the degree to which the job provides freedom to the individual doing work
  • Feedback:
    • IS the degree to which the individual knows how well they are performing

Ethics

  • Reflects the degree to which the behaviors of an authority are in accordance with generally accepted moral norms
  • Research on ethics seeks to explain why people behave in a manner consistent with generally accepted norms of morality and why people sometimes violate the norms
  • Morals:
    • Moral Awareness:
      • Occurs when an authority occurs when authority recognizes the moral issue exists
    • Moral Judgment:
      • When the authority accurately identifies the morally right course of action
    • Moral Intent:
      • Distinction between awareness or judgement because many unethical people know and understand that what they are doing is wrong
      • They just choose to do it anyways

  • unavoidable and certain

Trust

  • Defined as the willingness to be vulnerable to an authority based on positive expectations
    • This correlates to the Willingness to take a risk
  • Trust=willingness to be vulnerable
  • Risk=actually becoming vulnerable

Disposition based

  • Means the your personality includes a general propensity to trust others
  • Factors:
    • Personality
  • New relationships fall here
  • Has less to do with the authority figure and more to do with the truster
    • Some trustors are high in trust propensity meaning that they have an expectation of individuals promises and words can be trusted
  • This is pretty high in the United States compared to other countries

Cognition based

  • Means trust is rooted in a rational assessment of authority
  • Factors:
    • Competence
    • Character
    • Benevolence
  • Most relationships fall here
  • Trustworthiness is defined as the characteristics or attributes of a trustee that inspire trust
    • Driven by a track record
  • Track Record
    • Competence:
      • Defined as skills and ability that enable the authority to be successful
    • Character:
      • Perception that the authority adheres to a set of values and principles the trustor finds acceptable
    • Benevolence:
      • Defined as the belief that that authority wants to do good for the trustor

Affect based trust

  • Depends on feelings toward the authority that go beyond rationality
  • Factors:
    • Feelings towards the person
  • Few relationships ever reach this level
  • Leap of faith:
    • As emotional bonds develops out feelings for the trustee further increase out willingness to accept vulnerability

Justice

  • Reflects the perceived fairness of an authority's decisions

Distributive

  • Reflects fairness of decision making outcomes
  • Employees gauge distributive justice by asking whether decision outcomes such as rewards evaluations promotions and work assignments are allocated using proper norms
  • Rules:
    • Equity vs Equality vs Need:
      • Are rewards allocated according to the proper norm

Procedural

  • Reflects the fairness of the decision making process
  • This is fostered when authorities adhere to rules
  • Rules:
    • Voice:
      • Concerns giving employees a chance to express their opinions
      • Improves employees acceptingness of decisions
    • Correctability:
      • Provides employees with a chance to request an appeal when the system has worked poorly
    • Consistency:
      • Help ensure everything is neutral across all parties
    • Bias suppression
      • Help ensure everything is neutral across all parties
    • Representativeness
      • Help ensure everything is neutral across all parties
  • When outcomes are bad procedural justice becomes VERY important
  • Procedural justice is a better driver of reactions to authorities

Interpersonal Justice

  • Reflects perceived fairness of treatment received by employees from authorities

Informational Justice

  • Reflects perceived fairness in the communications provided to employees from authorities about decisions
  • Radio shack fired 400 people with an 18 word email and then gave them 30 min to gtfo
  • Effects of good and bad communication:
    • Long sincere explanations for example with pay cuts halves the rate of employee theft
    • Short impersonal explanation

Module 2

Stress

  • A psychological response to demands when there is something at stake, and coping with the demands taxes/exceeds are persons ability or resources
    • The particular demands that cause people to feel stress are called stressors
    • Examples of negative consequences that occur when people are over stressed:
      • Headaches
      • Heart attacks
      • depression
      • violent behavior
  • Why are some employees more stressed than others:
    • Constitutional factors
    • Early childhood development
    • Skill level, competence
    • Ego Strength
    • Healthy Lifestyles
    • Coping Methods

Stressors

  • Work/Hindrance
    • Role conflict:
      • Refers to conflicting expectations that other people may have of us
    • Role ambiguity:
      • Refers to the lack of information regarding what needs to be done in a role
      • Also the unpredictability of consequences for not performing
    • Role Overload:
      • Occurs when the number of demanding roles a person holds is so high that the person simply cannot perform some or all effectively
    • Daily hassles:
      • Minor day to day demands that get in the way of accomplishing the things that we want to accomplish:
        • Unnecessary paperwork
        • Useless communications
        • Bureaucratic
  • Work Challenge:
    • Time pressure:
      • Strong sense that the time you have is not enough to complete the work
    • Work Complexity:
      • Refers to the degree to which the requirements of the work tax or exceed the abilities of the person
    • Work responsibility:
      • Refers to the nature of obligations that a person has to others
  • Non-work/Hindrance:
    • Work-Family Conflict:
      • Demands at work or home hinder fulfilment of the demands in the other role
    • Negative life events:
      • Hinder ability to achieve life goals
  • Non-work/Challenge:
    • Family time demands:
      • Reflect time that a person commits to participate in an array of family activities
    • Personal development:
      • Participation in formal education programs, golf lessons, etc
    • Positive life events:
      • Marriage, children, grand children

General Adaptation Syndrome

  • Suggests that the body has a set of responses that allow it to adapt and function in the face of stress
  • Yerkes Dodson law suggests that there is an optimal stress level where performance is at its best

Stage 1 Alarm

  • Resistance to the stressors is temporarily lowered
  • Stressors sinks in
  • Body starts to resist and counteract the stressor

Stage 2 Resistance

  • Increased arousal of the mind and body helps the person respond and adapt to the demand

Stage 3 Exhaustion

  • Prolonged or repasted exposure to the stressor could cause body to break down:
    • Possibly causing illness or even death

Coping Strategies

  • Different coping strategies address different demands
  • People pick the strategy based on some degree of control they feel it gives them over the problem
  • Behavior:
    • Working harder
    • Seeking assistance
    • Getting more resources
    • Doing something else for a while
    • Seeking support
    • Venting Anger
  • Cognitive:
    • Strategizing
    • Self-motivation
    • Changing priorities
    • Avoiding the problem
    • Looking for the positive in the negative
    • Reappraising

      • Flex time

    • Health and wellness programs
  • What can you control:
    • Do something each day that gives you a sense of accomplishment:
      • Exercise
      • Learn
      • Read
    • Something you can say "At least I did that today"

Learned Helplessness

  • When lab animals were randomly shocked they learned to be helpless:
    • Because they thought they had no control over being shocked they stopped trying to avoid it
  • Could not unlearn the helpless behavior
  • Animals that could control the shocks were okay
  • Created model of clinical depression

Big 5 Traits

Conscientiousness

  • Conscientiousness has the biggest influence on job performance
  • Conscientious employees prioritize accomplishment striving
  • Traits
    • Dependable
    • Organized
    • Reliable
    • Hardworking
    • Persevering

Agreeableness

  • Prioritize communion striving:
    • Strong desire to obtain acceptance in personal relationships
    • Beneficial in some situation and detrimental in others
  • Focus on getting along, not necessarily getting ahead
  • Traits:
    • Warm
    • Kind
    • Cooperative
    • Sympathetic
    • Helpful
    • Courteous

Extraversion

  • Easiest to judge in zero acquaintance situations (Situations where both parties are strangers)
  • Prioritize status striving:
    • Strong desire to obtain power and influence within a social structure as a means of expressing personality
  • Tend to be high in positive affectivity
    • Dispositional tendency to experience pleasant and engaging moods
  • Traits
    • Talkative
    • Sociable
    • Passionate
    • Assertive
    • Bold
    • Dominant

Neuroticism

  • Synonymous with negative affectivity
    • A dispositional tendency to experience unpleasant moods
  • Associated with differential exposure
    • More likely to appraise day to day situations as stressful
  • Associate with differential reactivity
    • Less likely to believe they can cope with stress
  • Locus of control is typically external meaning they don't think they can influence events that effect them
  • Less neurotic people typically have more of an internal locus of control
  • Traits
    • Nervous
    • Moody
    • Emotional
    • Insecure
    • Jealous

Openness to experience

  • Also called inquisitiveness, Intellectualness, or even Culture
  • More likely to be valuable in jobs that require high levels of creativity
  • Highly open individuals are more likely to migrate into artistic and scientific fields
  • Traits:
    • Curious
    • Imaginative
    • Creative
    • Complex
    • Refined
    • Sophisticated

Myers-Briggs

  • MBTI evaluates individuals on basis of four types of preferences
  • Extraversion:
    • Being energized by people and social interactions
    • How extroverted are you compared to your levels of inter version
  • Sensing:
    • Proffering clear and concrete facts and data
    • Compare to how much intuition which is how much you like to infer data
  • Thinking:
    • Approaches decisions with logic and critical analysis
    • Compare to how much stock is put into "feeling" when making a decision (I.E. how much social influence is present in the decision making process)
  • Judging:
    • Approaches tasks by planning and goal setting
    • Compare to Perceiving (I.E. Winging it)

DISC

  • Dominance:
    • Relating to control power and assertiveness
  • Influence:
    • Social situations and communication
  • Steadiness:
    • Patience, persistence and thoughtfulness
  • Conscientiousness:
    • Structure and organization

RIASEC

  • Realistic:
    • Enjoy practical hands on tasks
  • Investigative:
    • Abstract analytical theory oriented tasks
  • Artistic:
    • Enjoy entertaining and fascinating other using imagination
  • Social:
    • Enjoy helping,serving or assisting others
  • Enterprising:
    • Enjoy persuading leading or outperforming others
  • Conventional:
    • Enjoy organizing counting or regulating people or things

BFOQ

  • Bona Fide Occupational Qualification
  • A necessary (not just proffered) qualification for performing a job
  • Supreme Court has ruled that BFOQ's are limited to policies directly related to a workers ability to do the job

Tests

  • Integrity Tests:
    • Focus specifically on predisposition to engage in theft and other counterproductive behaviors
    • More strongly related to job performance than conscientiousness tests
  • Clear purpose tests:
    • Asks applicants about their attitudes toward
      • Dishonesty
      • Beliefs about how frequent dishonesty
      • Endorsement of common rationalizations for dishonesty
      • Desire to punish dishonesty
    • Example:
      • Did you ever think about taking money from where you worked but didn't go through with it?
      • Is it OK to get around the law if you don't break it
      • If you were sent an extra item with an order would you send it back?
  • Veiled purpose tests:
    • Assess more general personality traits that are associated with dishonest acts
    • Example:
      • I like to plan things ahead
      • I've never hurt anyones feelings
      • I don't feel I have control over my life
  • About 1/3 of fortune 1000 companies plan to implement some form of personality testing
  • Almost everyone fakes on personality tests (Lies, or exaggerates on questions)
    • Since Almost everyone fakes to some degree correlations with outcomes like theft or other counterproductive behaviors are relatively unaffected

Workplace Violence

  • For women the number 1 cause of death in the work place is homicide
  • Form men the number 2 cause of death in the work place is homicide
  • Two types of violence:
    • First is where there are threats and intend to carry them out
    • Second is where there are threats and no intent to carry them out
  • Common myth is that someone just snaps:
    • It is rarely the case
  • It should be a workplace culture that threats should be taken seriously and neither parties will initially be in trouble until more investigating has been done

Get it early

  • First line of defense, be sensitive to your people and be aware and in touch:
    • Who is too wired
    • Who is too uptight
    • Just be aware of how all of your employees are feeling
  • Do you escalate or de-escalate situation:
    • Respect privacy
    • Use timing wisely
    • Know when to disengage:
      • Sometimes this is the smart move because you may just antagonise them angry person
    • If they are too upset, they don't listen:
      • If they are irrational it is not the time to deal with someone
    • Make appointments
      • This is good to get them in a more stable emotional state allowing you to talk about them rationally

Handling Emotions

  • First thing is to go to HR and let them know whats up
  • Start where the person is
  • Ask questions you don't want the answer to - Let them vent:
    • You are just gonna sit and listen:
      • At the end of the hour you are not supposed to have a solution they are supposed to come up with their own
    • Mark off time in your book with not interruptions, make it a priority
    • Example: Ask "What are you concerned about"
      • Phrases like this don't imply your opinion
  • Don't offer solutions or try and fix the problem:
    • If you try and offer solutions your invalidate their feelings
    • You can't fix emotions
    • DO NOT tell them how to feel

Managing the problem

  • If you don't directly deal with the problem then you allow the problem to come back and erupt:
    • You need to sit down and talk about the problem with the employee
    • Example:
      • Using layoffs a solution to remove the problem from the workplace
  • Intervention at an early stage has a high chance of stopping the escalation of the problem
  • Using a constant policy and procedure to deal with the problem:
    • Document the incident and started to determine if it should be reported
    • This is a strong justification to monitor and review the employee at the very least
  • Initial Response:
    • Interview Employee
    • Interview Supervisor
    • Interview Co-workers
  • Late stage intervention:
    • Encourage the reporting of threats
    • Convene management team to assess threats
    • Notify threated personal
    • Consider legal options
    • Develop short and long term security plan
  • Summary:
    • Be aware of the problem of workplace violence, and understand it is a priority
    • When early warning signals occur and document them
    • Enforce company policy that sends the message that the behavior is not acceptable
