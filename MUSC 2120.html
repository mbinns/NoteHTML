Music

  • Organized sound

Rhythm

  • Time in which music exists
  • Duration and arrangement of notes:
    • Beats its the count of one think second hand on a clock, doesn't usually change in a song, very boring
      • Whole
      • Quarter
      • Standard
      • Eighth note
    • Arrangement is how the beats and accents are put together
    • Accents is inflection put on a beat:
      • Example, making the first note of every three slightly louder (Meter)
      • They are commonly put on the downbeat (which is the first note in the pattern)
  • Syncopation:
    • Just breaking of the rules
      • For example not having an accented downbeat

Meter

  • A repeated pattern of accented and unaccented beats
  • Simple:
    • Duple (Not very common):
      • one, two
      • One typically has an accent
    • Triple (More common than duple):
      • one, two, three
      • One typically has an accent
  • Compound:
    • Quadruple Meter (Common Meter):
      • one, two, three, four
      • One typically has an accent, sometimes three has and accent
    • 6 beats:
      • 6 Eighth notes with one and four getting the accent
  • Odd meters do exist they are just harder to make work since they don't divide easily

Beats

  • Are typically divided up into measures

Tempo

  • Is the fast and slow of the beat
  • Typically given at the beginning of classical songs
  • Many common tempo terms are in Italian:
    • Larghissimo – very, very slow (24 bpm and under)
    • Grave – very slow (25–45 bpm)
    • Largo – broadly (40–60 bpm)
    • Lento – slowly (45–60 bpm)
    • Larghetto – rather broadly (60–66 bpm)
    • Adagio – slow and stately (literally, "at ease") (66–76 bpm)
    • Adagietto – slower than andante (72–76 bpm)
    • Andante – at a walking pace (76–108 bpm)
    • Andantino – slightly faster than Andante (although in some cases it can be taken to mean slightly slower than andante) (80–108 bpm)
    • Marcia moderato – moderately, in the manner of a march[7][8] (83–85 bpm)
    • Andante moderato – between andante and moderato (thus the name andante moderato) (92–112 bpm)
    • Moderato – moderately (108–120 bpm)
    • Allegretto – by the mid 19th century, moderately fast (112–120 bpm); see paragraph above for earlier usage
    • Allegro moderato – close to but not quite allegro (116–120 bpm)
    • Allegro – fast, quickly, and bright (120–168 bpm) (molto allegro is slightly faster than allegro, but always in its range)
    • Vivace – lively and fast (168–176 bpm)
    • Vivacissimo – very fast and lively (172–176 bpm)
    • Allegrissimo or Allegro vivace – very fast (172–176 bpm)
    • Presto – very, very fast (168–200 bpm)
    • Prestissimo – even faster than Presto (200 bpm and over)
  • To keep a tempo precisely you should use a metronome
  • Rapid or abrupt changes in tempo are rare
  • Gradual changes in tempo do happen:
    • Accelerando - Means to get faster gradually
    • Ritardando - Means to get slower gradually
    • Typically accompanied by a change in dynamics:
      • Dynamics are the change in volume
