Trade

  • Wealth of societies is maximized when people are allowed to specialize and trade
  • Absolute Advantage:
    • Is when an individual/country can produce a good using fewer resources
    • Who ever can produce more of a good in a time frame will have the absolute advantage
  • Comparative Advantage:
    • Is when an individual/country can produce a good at lower opportunity cost
    • To find who has the comparative advantage you must calculate opportunity cost for each producer
    • You cannot have the comparative advantage in all goods
    • The Law of Comparative Advantage:
      • People and countries should specialize in what they have a comparative advantage in and then trade for other goods
  • Example:
    • Snoop dog
      • can produce 12 bottles of gin
      • 36 bottles of juice
      • Or any linear combo of the two
    • Snoop Lion:
      • con produce 10 bottles of gin
      • can produce 5 bottles of juice
    • Absolute advantage:
      • Snoop dog has the absolute advantage in both juice and gin because given the time frame he can produce more of each
    • Comparative advantage:
      • Snoop dog:
        • OC of 1gin = 36juice/12gin = 3juice per bottle of gin
        • OC of 1juice = 12gin/36juice = 1/3 bottle of gin per juice **
      • Snoop Lion:
        • OC of 1gin = 5juice/10gin = 1/2juice per bottle of gin **
        • OC of 1juice = 10gin/5juice= 2 bottles of gin per juice
      • Snoop Lion has the comparative advantage in Gin because he gives up production of less juice per bottle of gin therefore Snoop Lion should make Gin **
      • Snoop Dog has the comparative advantage in Juice because he gives up production of less gin per bottle of juice therefore Snoop Dog should make Juice **
    • In Trade:
      • The MOST Dog will pay for gin in 3 juices because any more than that and it would be better for him to make it
      • The MOST Lion will pay for juice is 2 Gin because any more than that and it would be better for him to make it
  • PPF:
    • Production possibility frontier is a graphical representation of the combination of goods that an individual/country can produce given limited resources
    • If you are producing on the line between two points you are producing "efficiently"

Equilibrium

  • Also called the market clearing price
  • Out of equilibrium surplus
  • Surplus:
    • The quantity supplied exceeds the quantity demanded
  • Price:
    • If there is a surplus producers will raise the price (if they legally can) until a equilibrium is met
    • If there is a shortage consumers will raise the price (if they legally can) until a equilibrium is met
  • Equilibrium price - price at which the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied:
    • this price maximises gains from trade
    • this price allocates good to those who values them most
  • Supply curve, can also be thought of as the opportunity cost curve

Elasticity

  • Two types:
    • Demand elasticity:
      • Is not equal to the slope of the demand curve
      • Don't use the midpoint method
      • Elasticity:
    • Supply elasticity

  • If elasticity is equal to 1 then it is called "unit elastic"
  • Inelastic demand:
    • The quantity response is small compared to the change in price
    • Steeper slopes
  • Elastic:
    • The quantity response is large compared to the change in price
    • flatter slopes

Determinants of elasticity

  • Number of substitutes an item has makes it more elastic:
    • If coke and Pepsi are the same in your mind and are the exact same price:
      • If they raise the price of coke:
        • You will buy Pepsi
      • You would be very responsive to the increase or decrease in price of one or the other
    • The more substitutes something has the more responsive you will be to price changes (steeper slopes)
  • Necessity/non-necessity
    • Have a more inelastic demand curve
    • Non-necessity (luxury) goods will have a more inelastic curve
  • Demand for drugs tend to be inelastic:
    • They do not have close substitutes since many drugs are patented
    • They are also necessities to those who are using them for treatment
  • Time periods
    • Short term: Less elastic (more inelastic):
      • Less time to figure out other options
    • Long term: More elastic:
      • More time to figure out other options
  • Good classifications
  • Budget Share:
    • What percentage of your total income do you spend on a good:
      • Small part:
        • Less elastic
      • Large part:
        • More elastic
    • Price of bread increases:
      • Low income households will reduce their consumption of bread by a greater percentage than high income households based on price fluctuation
    • Less responsive to price changes when the good is a small part of your budget
  • Perfect inelastic goods:
    • No matter the price you will buy the same quantity
    • Strait up and down line
    • No substitutes
  • Perfect elastic
    • Infinitely many substitutes
    • Horizontal line (no slope)
