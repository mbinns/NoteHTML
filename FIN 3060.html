Chapter 1

CFO

Functions

  • Capitol planning
    • Different projects the company plans on doing
      • Ex.) replacing all computers with new computers
  • Determines the risks and cost opportunity to see if a decision is financially sound
  • Handling short/long term debts and bonds

Business Orgs

Sole proprietorship

  • You are the only owner
  • Easy to start
  • Unlimited liability:
    • They can come after EVERYTHING you own
  • Low liquidity

Partnership

  • You and someone else
  • Unincorporated
  • General partnership:
    • All partners work in the business
    • All partners have Joint unlimited liability
  • Limited partner:
    • Not active in the business
    • Typically just provide money
    • Limited liability
  • Sec dual 10k
  • Low liquidity

The Corporation

  • Separate legal person under the law and has all the rights of a person:
    • A "Legal Entity"
  • Delaware is a popular spot to be incorporated
  • Must have letters of incorporation
  • Typically seed corps have stock and elect a board of directors
  • Corporation has an unlimited life because shares
  • Corporate liability shield:
    • The corporation acts as a single person and the shareholders are not on the hook for actions
  • Disadvantages:
    • C Corporations are taxed twice:
      • Must pay corporate taxes of 35%:
        • Countered buy raising the price of goods
      • And individual taxes if they pay dividends
  • S corporations do not pay federal taxes:
    • Fewer than 100 shareholders

Limited Liability Company

  • Operates and is taxed 1 time like a partnership
  • Limited liability
  • Who?
    • Law firms
    • Hedge funds
    • etc.

Management interest vs Stockholder

  • Stock holders do not control the corporation management does:
    • This introduces the agency problem:
      • When one group hires another to run the organization
  • Financial management can help through:
    • Incentive stock options by giving managers stock options to help curb fighting takeovers
    • Bonuses:
      • Some downsides are managers may focus short-term rather than long term
  • Poorly managed companies are good take over candidates

Proxy Wars

  • Where managers send out requests to vote on behalf of the shareholders
  • Shareholders don't like that and they send their own requests out to vote in opposition if they don't like the management

Stakeholders

  • Not shareholders
  • Examples:)
    • Creditors
    • Suppliers

Financial Market

  • What is bought and sold in the market:
    • Financial instruments
    • Bonds
    • Stocks
    • Credit
  • What is raised?
    • Short term debt
    • Long term debt
    • Equity

Primary Market

  • Original sale of securities
  • Company goes to sell its bonds and the money goes to who ever owns the bonds/stocks:
    • Can be through a public offering:
      • Must be register through SEC which is expensive and long
    • Private placement:
      • Skip public offering and go to where large pools of money are:
        • Insurance firms
        • Pension funds
        • ETC.

Secondary Market

  • Exchange happens from one investor to another investor and nothing goes to the original bond owner I.E. the company
  • Very active, this complements the primary market

Dealer market

  • Think auto dealer
  • The people own what they are selling you, selling from their own accounts
  • They have to buy at the bid and sell at ask price:
    • Profit is the difference
  • No real physical market just an interconnected distributed network

Auction market

  • They don't own their own stock
  • Matching buyers and sellers
  • Generally has a physical location

Chapter 2

Balance Sheet

  • Comprised of assets:
    • What we own
    • Fixed or current:
      • Current can be converted into cash in less than a year
    • Assets = Own Equity + Liabilities
  • Liabilities:
    • What we owe
  • Owners equity:
    • Common stock par value:
      • The par value of stock bought x number
    • Paid in surplus:
      • Amount paid over par value x number
    • retained earnings:
      • Not paid out dividends
    • Treasury stock:
      • Company buys their own stock back at cheaper price
      • This kicks earnings per share up because there is less stocks to divide things up between
      • You can also issue preferred stock options out of the treasury stocks
  • Decisions:
    • Investment Decisions:
      • What are we going to buy
    • Finance Decisions:
      • Issuing stock
      • Issuing dividends
      • Etc.

Things you can learn from bal sheet

Firms liquidity

  • Liquidity trade-off:
    • The higher liquidity of an item the less returns you typical get back from it
  • Assets are listed in order of liquidity:
    • Book value != Market value
    • Examples:
      • Cash
      • Treasury bill
  • How fast things can be sold
  • How close to the current cash price can they be sold for in a time frame
  • Loss of value:
    • How much money will you lose if you have to sell something RIGHT NOW
    • Example:
      • Houses could take 6th months to sell to make cash back but it could be sold right now for half price

Capitol Structure

  • Useful for investors and debtors
  • Where does the money come from
    • Owners equity or debt

Debt

  • Financial leverage:
    • If things are going well borrowing money can boost profits
    • If things are going poorly you will lose money because the interest is higher than the extra profit made from borrowing

Income Statement

  • Net Income:
    • Rev - Expense = Income
    • Start with sales
    • less Cost of goods sold
    • less depreciation
    • = Earnings before taxes (EBIT)
    • less interest
    • = Taxable income
    • less taxes
    • = Net Income or Earn After Taxes (EAT)
    • Either payed out as dividends or reinvested as Retained Earnings
  • Rev and Expense cash flows don't necessarily reflect the actual cash flow
  • Some cash flows don't actually have cash associated with it like depreciation
  • Life spans:
    • Economic lifespan:
      • How long will it make you money
    • Useful lifespan:
      • How long did the IRS say you can depreciate the machine over

Tax rate

  • Average v Marginal Tax rate:
    • Average = Taxes/Taxable Income:
      • Used to calc how much of the income will be taxed in incremental brackets
    • Marginal = how much tax would you pay on an extra dollar based on the tax bracket the dollar is in
      • Marginal should be used to perform forecasting and capitol budgeting projections

Cash Flow Identity

Capitol Spending:

  • Spending on long term fixed assets
  • Net Capitol Spending = Money spent on Fixed assets - Sales of Fixed assets
  • \(NCS = Ending Fixed Assets - (Beginning Fixed Assets + Dep)\)
  • \(NCS = Purchase Fixed Assets - Sale Fixed Assets\)
  • High NCS could be an indicator of whether to invest or not:
    • Want to find out why they had an increase or decrease in NCS
    • If they have an increase in NCS you want to see an increase in Net Working Capitol
  • Can be negative if more are sold then bought:
    • Increases cash flow from assets

Operating Cash Flow:

  • Accounting OCF:
    • \(NETinc + Depreciation\)
    • Accountants consider liabilities and interest paid as OCF:
      • These must be removed from the calculation
  • Finance OCF:
    • Starts with EBIT:
      • Before interest is deducted
      • Interest is taken into account when setting the discount rate
  • Difference between Finance OCF and Accounting OCF is interest

Cash flow paid to creditors

  • People we owe money to
  • We pay interest to creditors:
    • \(InterestPaid - NetNewLTD\)

Cash Flow Paid To Shareholders

  • Paid dividends to shareholders
  • \(CFPTS = DivPaid - NetNewCap\)
  • \(NetNewCap= (Common Stock + Paid in Surplus) \div (EOY - BOY)\)
  • Retained earnings are not added because its reinvested into the company and not paid out

Cash Flow From Assets

  • A growing company typically has a negative cash flow from assets because they are expanding rapidly and are taking on new debit
  • They may have a big Capitol Spending Project like a new more modern factory:
    • That would be an okay reason to have a negative flow
  • They may have to comply with gov't regulations:
    • Won't make the company any more money
    • Wouldn't be a good reason to have a negative flow

Chapter 3

Ratios

Short Term Solvency

  • Liquidity:
    • Highly liquid assets can be converted to cash quickly with out a significant loss of money
    • Non-Liqud Assets cannot be converted to cash quickly with out a significant loss of money
  • We are looking to see if the company can pay its bills in the next year
  • Short term creditors are interested in this ration
  • 3 Ratios:
    • Current Ratio:
      • \(\frac{Cur\ Assets} {Cur\ Liab}\)
      • This might not a good thing because the returns are low unless the company plans on using it quickly
      • Want the ratio to be at least one:
        • If the ratio is less than one you have more debt then money to pay it
      • If you sell some merchandise out of inventory your Curr ratio will go up
    • Quick Ratio:
      • \(\frac{Cur\ Assets - Inv} {Cur\ Liab}\)
      • Shows how much of their money is in inventory
      • Could mean a few things:
        • The company may not be selling their inventory
        • The company may have over produced
        • This typically means you should ask questions about the inventory
      • If you sell current inventory for cash then then quick ratio will decrease
        • Because your inventory and current assets don't mirror one another in this ratio
    • Cash Ratio:
      • \(\frac{Cash} {Cur\ Liabs}\)
      • How much cash they have on hand compared to how much debt they have that may need to be paid

Long Term Solvency

  • Can the company meet its long term debt obligations
  • Tell the relative ammount of long term

Total Debit Ratio:

  • Total Debt Ratio:
    • Debt vs total assets
    • \(\frac{total\ debit} {Total\ assets}\)
    • \(\frac{total\ assets - total\ eq} {Total\ assets}\)
    • \(\frac{STD + LTD} {Total\ assets}\)
  • Debt/equity:
    • Creditors supply vs Owners (shareholders supply)
    • \(\frac{total\ debit} {Total\ EQ}\)
    • \(\frac{STD + LTD} {Total\ EQ}\)
  • Equity multiplier:
    • Total assets vs total equity
    • \(1 + \frac{debt} {eq}\)
    • \(\frac{total\ assets} {Total\ EQ}\)

Times Interest Earned

  • How many times does earnings before interest cover interest expense
  • \(\frac{EBIT}{Intrest\ Expense}\)
  • Cash Coverage Ratio:
    • \(\frac{EBIT + Depretiation}{Intrest\ Expense}\)

Turnover Ratios

  • Inventory Turnover
    • How many times you turn over your inventory
    • Typically good to have higher turn over ratio here as long as your inventory is not running out and you are missing sales
    • \(\frac{COGS}{Inv}\)
    • How long does something sit in inventory:
      • \(\frac{365}{Inv\ Turnover\ Ratio}\)
  • Receivables Turn Over:
    • How long on averse does it take to collect from customers
    • \(\frac{Sales}{Acct\ Rev}\)
    • How long till customers pay
      • \(\frac{365}{Acct\ Rec\ Turnover\ Ratio}\)
  • Accounts payable turn over:
    • \(\frac{COGS}{Acct\ pay}\)
    • How long till suppliers are payed
      • \(\frac{365}{Acct\ Pay\ Turnover\ Ratio}\)
  • Total Asset Turnover:
    • \(\frac{Sales}{T.assets}\)
    • For every $ in assets we generated X dollars in sales
  • Capital intensity ratio:
    • It takes X in assets to create X in sales:
      • This could be a low ratio because all of the equipment is super old and not worth much
      • A high ratio could indicate that the equipment is new and also they may have lots of capital spending projects in the works
    • \(\frac{1}{T.Assets\ Turn over}\)

Profitability ratio

  • How effectively does the company manage its assets

Profit Margin

  • P generates X numbers of Dollars
  • \(\frac{Net\ Inc}{Sales}\)

Return on Assets

  • Gives profit per $ of assets
  • \(\frac{Net\ T.Eq}{Sales}\)
  • If return on equity is greater than return on assets it means you are using debt for leverage:
  • Which will only work for you if the cost of debit is less than the return on equity

Return on Equity

  • Increasing ROE:
    • The SGR and IGR increases
    • Increase the profit margin
      • Increases the ability to generate funds internally
    • Increase asset turn over
    • Increase debt/equity ratio:
      • You can grow up to a point
      • Be careful because you don't want interest to exceed return on assets
    • Decreasing Dividends paid out / Or the rate of increase:
      • Decreasing the rate of increase is the best way
      • Decreasing pay out will cause stock price to drop
    • Sell new shares of stock
  • \(\frac{Net\ T.Eq}{EQ}\)

Market Value

  • What does the market value the stock at

Earnings per share

  • \(\frac{Net\ Inc}{Stock Outstanding}\)
  • Shares outstanding is shares issued - treasury stock

Price earnings ration

  • Tells us the market rate discount on the stock
  • \(\frac{Price\ Per\ Share}{EPS}\)

Market to book ratio

  • \(\frac{MKT\ Val\ Per\ Sh}{Book\ Val\ Per\ Sh}\)

Dividend payout ratio

  • \(\frac{Div}{Net\ inc}\)

Retention Ratio

  • Text calls retention ratio 'b'
  • Also called plowback ratio
  • \(\frac{Addition\ to\ RE}{Net\ Inc}\)

Growth Rates

Internal Growth Rate

  • Internal growth rate is based on return on assets
  • \(\frac{Return\ on\ assets * b}{1-(Return\ on\ assets * b)}\)

Sustainable Growth Rate

  • Based on return on equity, which allows for the inclusion of some debt
  • \(\frac{Return\ on\ equity * b}{1-(Return\ on\ assets * b)}\)
